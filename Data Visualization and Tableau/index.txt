Dashboards: 
Enables analysis, monitoring, measurement and comparison of data.

Every dashboard will be created for a certain purpose.
The data displayed and the graphs displayed will be chosen according to the purpose of the dashboard.
The design and layout of the dashboard should also match the purpose of the dashboard.

Main Dashboard- will contain links and summery to all the dashboards.

Design Rules- As with any visualization, there are design rules that will help make the dashboard more understandable, and will convey the message better:
- Text - size, color, bold
- Color
- Frame
- Position & Hierarchy

Interactivity - Customers can be allowed to perform actions that will affect the display of data on the dashboard:
- ToolTip
- Filter
- Zoom 


Data Visualization Principles: 
- Audience: Matching the message and graph to the audience, How can the audience's attention be drawn?
- Message: Easy to understand, Transfers the conclusions from the raw data. 
Promotes processes and actions, Tells a story!
- Data: Display only necessary information, Simplify less important information.
Each part of the graph should have a connection to the information and message.
No unnecessary drawings or texts.
Ask yourself - why am I using this information? 
For what purpose? 
- Choosing the Right Graph: Consider the variables, Consider the need.
Try different graphs and consult with colleagues.
- Titles and Labels (Headline): The first thing the audience sees in the graph.
Should be clear, simple and convey the purpose of the graph.
Axes labels (including units of measure if required), Legend if necessary.
A study examined and found that good headlines affect audience memory, and their understanding of the data.
- Axis: For most charts, the numerical axis (usually the y axis) must start at zero.
Our eyes are very sensitive to the area of bars, and we will draw inaccurate conclusions when the bars are truncated
The axis should be continuous - no skipping values, Skipping values in the axis may cause a distortion of the trend.
- Color: To emphasize the important details.
To show the difference between variables.
To represent quantities / volume.
Use colors according to their meaning ( Dark vs Bright).
The colors in the graph must match each other!

Different colors should be used for different categories (male/female, types of fruit), 
not different values in a range (age, temperature).
In order to show colors of a numerical range use a sequential range and not a rainbow range
When choosing colors, remember that some viewers have color-blindness

Use the same colors, axes, and labels across multiple charts.
In the same presentation, we will be consistent when using a color to represent a category.
Do not use more than six (6) colors in a visualization


Gestalt Principles - Principles that describe how humans group similar elements, recognize patterns and simplify complex images when we perceive objects:
- Closure
- Similarity
- Proximity
- Symmetry & Order
- Common Fate
- Continuation
